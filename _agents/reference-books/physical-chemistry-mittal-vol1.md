## Chapter 1 — "Basics About Chemistry" (book pp. 1–22) — section map

- **1.0 Chemistry: The Science of Matter** — inquiry opening: "look around your desk — scratch the paper, the pencil, the paper clip, the glass bottle; burn equal pieces; which is harder/heavier?" Chemistry as rooted in everyday curiosity (why...? how...?).
- **1.1 What is Science?** — biological vs physical sciences; chemistry as the "central" science applying physics + maths.
- **1.2 How Does Science Work?** — the scientific method: **law → hypothesis → theory**. Timeline table of foundational laws (1774 Conservation of Mass→Dalton; 1662 Boyle→KTG; 1834 Faraday→ionic theory…), grouped into Laws of Chemical Combination vs Gas Laws.
- **1.3 What is Chemistry?** — principles (hypotheses/laws/theories) vs descriptive chemistry; keep a balance.
- **1.4 Subdivisions of Chemistry** — table of 5 branches (Physical, Organic, Inorganic, Analytical, Biochem); "central science"; blurring boundaries; specialised bridges (geo-, medicinal, polymer…).
- **1.5 Scope of Chemistry** — preconceptions; chemistry beyond test tubes; pollution, drugs, materials; "central science".
- **1.6 How to Study Chemistry** — ★ chemistry = **learning a new language** (specialised vocabulary), concepts are abstract but conquerable with diligence. Tips: attend + take notes; review the same day; think critically + explain a concept to a classmate; ask the teacher. Encouraging close: "much more than numbers and formulas... a logical discipline brimming with ideas."
- **1.7 Classification of Matter** — states (solid/liquid/gas, interconversion); **1.7.1** substances vs mixtures (homo/heterogeneous); **1.7.2** elements & compounds + symbols table (Latin names: Au/Fe/Na); **1.7.3** fundamental defs — physical vs chemical properties & changes; **1.7.4** alloys & amalgam; **1.7.5** non-stoichiometric compounds (FeO, Cu₂O, the YBa₂Cu₃O₇₋ₓ superconductor); **1.7.6** composites.
- **1.8 Units of Measurement** — metric vs SI table; **1.8.1 Length** (meter, cm; meterstick vs yardstick; 1 m = 39.4 in; 2.54 cm = 1 in); **1.8.2 Volume** (litre, mL); **1.8.3 Mass** (gram, kg; mass vs weight with the Earth/Moon astronaut; beam balance); **1.8.4 Temperature** (Celsius/Fahrenheit/Kelvin, absolute zero).
- **1.9 Scientific Notation** — width of a human hair (0.000008 m) vs hairs on a scalp (100000); powers of 10; coefficient × 10ⁿ.
- **1.10 Measured and Exact Numbers** — ★ reading a ruler; the **estimated last digit** & uncertainty (4.5 vs 4.55 vs 3.0 cm); exact numbers (counting / defined equalities).
- **1.11 Significant Figures** — ★ **accuracy vs precision** (the "four students measure your height / pizza" analogy); counting rules; sig figs in calculations; rounding; mult-div vs add-sub rules; significant zeros — all with worked cases.
- **1.12 SI and Metric Prefixes** — DV-of-nutrients table; prefix table mega→nano; **1.12.1 measuring length**, **1.12.2 volume** (cm³ = cc = mL), **1.12.3 mass** with prefixes.
- **1.13 Conversion Factors** — ★ dimensional analysis as a **method**: every equality → two factors; the 4-step scaffold **Given → Unit Plan → Relationship → Setup**; unit cancellation; *"if the units don't cancel, you made an error"*; metric–metric, metric–US, chaining 2+ factors, **percents as conversion factors** (body-fat %).
- **1.14 Mass and Energy** — E = mc²; mass–energy equivalence; mass defect (too small to weigh in ordinary reactions); "matter is bottled energy"; conservation of mass+energy.

## Style & voice guide — what makes Mittal's teaching good (borrow this)

1. **Inquiry-first openings.** Sections begin with an everyday scene or a question, *before* the definition ("Look around your desk… scratch them, burn them, which is harder?"). Hook → then formalise.
2. **Relentless real-life / clinical anchoring.** Measurement is taught through the nurse recording your mass/height/temperature, blood values in dL, IV bags in mL, body-fat %, cholesterol in mg/dL. Abstract units become tangible. (We tend to stay abstract — copy this.)
3. **Analogies as a named feature.** Hard ideas get a concrete analogy, often Indian/sporting (cricket & Vivian Richards for activation energy). Systematise: every abstract concept earns one analogy.
4. **The "Check" habit.** Worked solutions end with a *Check* that cross-verifies the answer — explicitly because "no answer key exists in the exam." Teach students to self-verify.
5. **Systematic problem-solving scaffold.** Numerical method is always: Given → Unit Plan → Relationship/Conversion factors → Setup (with unit cancellation) → Check. Unit cancellation is shown, and wrong setups are shown failing ("6215 miles²/day — wrong units!").
6. **Vocabulary-as-language framing.** Treats chemistry as a new language to be learned word by word; bolds each key term on first use; defs in italics.
7. **Two-tier exercises.** *Concept Building Exercise* (fully solved, teaches the method) immediately followed by *Concept Testing Exercise* (graded practice), then end-of-chapter Exercise-1 (conceptual/single-correct) and Exercise-2 (JEE pattern: multi-correct, passages, matching). Pull questions from both tiers.
8. **Honest about limits.** States approximations and where a model breaks, rather than over-claiming.
9. **Pictorial/flowchart representation** of multi-step procedures (e.g. the empirical-formula determination flowchart). Good candidates for our diagrams/sims.
10. **Optional "Boxes"** of fascinating tangents (e.g. the thermodynamic "drinking bird" toy) — flagged as skippable light reading. Maps to our `fun_fact` / `threads_of_curiosity` callouts.
