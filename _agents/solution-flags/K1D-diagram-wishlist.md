## 🅑 No current diagram — student would benefit from a sketch (high priority)

These are pure-text questions where a setup picture would help students *visualise* before computing:

### Free-fall and projectile setups
- **K1D-019** — Body falling under gravity covers points A and B (80m, 2s) — would benefit from a vertical-axis sketch showing start, A, B, and ground with annotated velocities and distances.
- **K1D-020** — Monkey-mango on a tree with NCC cadet walking below — classic "two-object timing" picture. A side view showing tree, mango drop trajectory, and cadet's path would make the matching condition obvious.
- **K1D-022** — Two balls dropped from a 180m tower at different times — would benefit from a vertical tower diagram showing both balls and the meeting point at 100m.
- **K1D-062** — Two stones from a tall building (5m and 25m starting points) — a vertical building diagram with both stones, their starting heights, and the simultaneous-ground-impact moment would clarify the head-start concept.
- **K1D-076** — Two balls thrown up at staggered times (t=0 and t=2s) — a height-vs-time plot showing both trajectories crossing at the meeting moment would be powerful.
- **K1D-079** — Balloon rising, then object dropped — a sketch showing the balloon at release height, the object's upward-then-downward path, and the balloon's continued ascent would visualise the "inherited velocity" insight.
- **K1D-110** — Two balls thrown up at staggered times (interval 3s) — same as above; a parabolic-trajectories diagram showing both balls and the collision height.
- **K1D-115** — Three water drops in mid-fall from a 9.8m nozzle — a side-view picture showing the three drops at different heights below the nozzle would crystallise the equal-time-interval idea.
- **K1D-114** — Up/down/dropped from a tower — three vertical trajectories shown side-by-side would make the geometric-mean relation feel natural.

### Two-vehicle / relative motion setups
- **K1D-032** — Two cars approaching, brake, gap remaining — a top-down sketch with stopping distances marked would make the "halve approach distance" intuition clear.
- **K1D-067** — Helicopter drops object during horizontal flight — a parabolic trajectory diagram (horizontal velocity + vertical fall) would help visualise the symmetric $2\,\text{km} \times 2\,\text{km}$ legs.
- **K1D-099** — Two trains entering a tunnel from opposite ends — top-down diagram of the tunnel, with both trains, their lengths, and the cross-time formula would help.
- **K1D-100** — Passenger in train A observes train B — relative-motion picture would help students see that the **length of B** is what determines the observation time.

### Kinematic / phase-space setups
- **K1D-031** — Body enters a constant force field along z, motion decoupled — a 3D coordinate-axis sketch with initial velocity in xy-plane and force along z would make the component-independence visible.
- **K1D-046** — "Car turning east-to-north at constant speed" — a top-down sketch showing the L-shaped path with initial velocity vector (east) and final velocity vector (north) would make the "speed unchanged but velocity changed" point click.
- **K1D-066** — Bullet through a mud wall — a side-view picture with wall thickness, initial KE, and resistive force F would help.
- **K1D-060/108** — Bullet penetration (variant problems) — a side-view picture with first-4-cm marker and total-stopping-distance marker would help students see "additional penetration".

### Phase-2 of conceptual setups (no current image)
- **K1D-086** — Bullet shot down + inelastic ground impact: the *correct option* shows the v-t graph. But the question doesn't currently have a diagram of the physical setup (height, initial downward velocity). A sketch would help.
- **K1D-117** — Tennis ball bouncing off floor: the *correct option* shows v-vs-h. But a side-view sketch of the bouncing ball with marked heights ($h$ and $h/2$) would orient students to the physical picture before they read the graph options.
- **K1D-118** — Steel ball in glycerine: a side-view of the cylinder with the ball settling toward terminal velocity would help.
- **K1D-120** — Constant acceleration: a side-by-side trio of x-t, v-t, a-t graphs would orient students. (The *option* shows this, but a tutorial-style "standard motion package" diagram in the solution sketch could help.)
- **K1D-121** — Constant power: an s-t sketch alongside the standard "constant force" comparison would highlight the $t^{3/2}$ vs $t^2$ difference.

## Notes for the diagram artist

- Background dark (`#050505`), white strokes/text per the question-bank convention.
- Match the visual style of existing K1D figures (already-present diagrams listed in section 🅐).
- For vertical-motion problems, use a left-hand vertical axis with downward as the natural fall direction.
