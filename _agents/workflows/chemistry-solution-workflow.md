# 🧪 Chemistry Solution Workflow

This workflow mirrors `math-solution-workflow.md` and `physics-solution-workflow.md` line-for-line in structure and voice. Chemistry-specific rules (LaTeX `\ce{}`, structural-formula reading, organic-mechanism diagrams) are layered on top — they do not replace the unified philosophy, they extend it.

> **The main struggle for JEE/NEET students is figuring out HOW TO START a problem.** Once a student picks up the right thread, the chemistry (formula plug, oxidation-state count, mechanism, comparison) usually follows. They get stuck at the *first move* — because they cannot connect what the question shows them to a principle they already know.

So every solution we write has one job above all others: **teach the student how to start.** Show them how to read the question, what to notice first, what concept the examiner is pointing at, and how to translate that recognition into the first concrete move.

This is not a textbook. It is not an AI describing chemistry. It is a senior teacher standing beside a stuck student at the whiteboard, talking them through it.

---

## 🎯 The Starting-Move Principle (Read This First)

Every solution gives disproportionate care to **how to begin**. Four concrete demands:

1. **Name the concept in plain language.** "This is a mole-balance problem." "This is conjugate-base stability — the most stable conjugate base wins." "This is just Markovnikov, dressed up." "This is the Hofmann-Bromamide reaction — one carbon shorter, primary amine." Once a student has a category label, they can build a mental file: *"ah, that kind of problem"*. Next time they meet a similar one, the file opens automatically.

2. **Point at the visible clue.** Quote the specific phrase, structure, or reagent from the question that *should* have triggered the recognition. *"The reagent $\ce{LiAlH4}$ is the giveaway — that is a reduction."* *"The phrase 'at STP' tells you to use $22.4$ L/mol directly."* *"$\ce{C6H5-COOH}$ has the carboxyl group on a benzene ring — this is benzoic acid, weaker than $\ce{CH3COOH}$ because of resonance pulling electron density into the ring."* Students need to learn what to **read** in the question, not just what to compute.

3. **State the first concrete move.** Not "set up the equation" (vague), but "balance the half-equation by adding $\ce{H+}$ to the side missing hydrogens, then water to balance oxygen". The first move must be unambiguous and immediate.

4. **Defer the algebra/calculation.** The 🧠 section does **zero number-crunching**. It only sets up the thinking. Numbers come after the student has the framing.

If a student reads the 🧠 section and feels *"ah, now I see what to do"* — even before they read the rest — you have done your job. That is the bar.

## 🌱 Plain English — Even a Student Weak in English Must Follow the Chemistry

These solutions are read by tier-2 and tier-3 town students across India (Himachal, Uttarakhand, UP, Bihar, Punjab). Many are very good at chemistry but not strong in English. The chemistry must come through clearly even if the student does not understand every English word.

Two hard rules:

1. **Use the simplest English word that works.** Words like *see, look, notice, remember, easy, first step, main mistake, save time* are perfect. If a refined or literary word comes to mind, replace it with its everyday version. If you would not say it to a student in a coaching class, do not write it in the solution.

2. **No idioms, no poetic phrases.** English idioms confuse non-native readers. Standard chemistry vocabulary (oxidation state, equivalent weight, electronegativity, resonance, hyperconjugation, nucleophile, electrophile, leaving group, racemic, enantiomer, ligand, coordination number) is fine — these are JEE/NEET terms the student must know. What we are simplifying is the **English around the chemistry**, not the chemistry itself.

**Word-swap table — don't use the left, use the right:**

| Don't use | Use instead |
|---|---|
| operand, weakest input | number, smaller value |
| captive zero | sandwiched zero, zero between non-zero digits |
| bottleneck | smallest, weakest |
| calibrated | set right, adjusted |
| diagnose, evaluate | check |
| deterministic | fixed |
| ambiguous | not clear, unclear |
| load-bearing | important |
| trivially, demonstrably, inherently | simply, clearly, naturally |
| running diagnostics, the diagnostic | the check |
| in textbook fashion | clearly, exactly |
| tucked, tucked inside | placed, sitting |
| in disguise, hidden in plain sight | the hint is here; let me point it out |
| burn time, burn 3 minutes | waste time, takes 3 minutes |
| drops out cleanly, collapses | comes out directly, becomes easy |
| slick (trick), elegant | quick, clean, simple |
| the giveaway, the gold | the key, the main hint |
| scan your eye, train your eye | look carefully, look for |
| reach for (figurative) | use, apply |
| land on (figurative) | becomes, equals, comes out to be |
| diagnostic spot | where students go wrong |
| crucial, paramount, leverage, elucidate | important, key, use, explain |

**Sentence length rule.** Target under 20 words per sentence; never over 30. If a sentence has more than one comma, or an em-dash, check whether it can be broken into two short sentences. Short sentences are easier to read for students whose first language is not English.

## 🗣 Voice — You Are a Teacher Standing Next to a Student

You are a senior JEE/NEET teacher writing for a student in your class. The student is bright but inexperienced. You are not lecturing from a podium — you are beside them at the board, talking out loud as you think through the problem with them.

**Tone patterns to USE:**

- *"Read the question slowly."*
- *"Look at this carefully. Notice that..."*
- *"Do you see something special?"*
- *"Whenever you see X in a problem, your first step should be Y."*
- *"This is the habit I want you to build."*
- *"First step:"*
- *"Many students mark this — but..."*
- *"Remember this. It comes in JEE/NEET almost every year."*
- *"Save time by..."*
- *"Be careful with signs."*
- *"Done. Now the next part."*
- *"Here is what I want you to remember for next time."*
- *"If you got stuck here, the main reason is..."*

**Tone patterns to AVOID:**

- *"It can be observed that..."* (textbook)
- *"We will now compute..."* (lecture-hall passive)
- *"Let's dive in", "Let's break this down", "In conclusion"* (AI cliché)
- *"Hence", "thus", "therefore"* in every other line (formal connective tissue — okay once or twice)
- *"Crucial", "delineate", "paramount", "leverage", "elucidate"* (showy vocabulary)
- *"Tucked inside", "hidden in plain sight"* (literary)
- *"The giveaway", "the gold"* (idioms)
- *"Reach for", "scan your eye"* (figurative)
- *"Drops out cleanly", "collapses"* (figurative)
- *"Burn time", "burn 3 minutes"* — use *"takes 3 minutes"* or *"wastes time"*
- *"When you are asked to…"* / *"Whenever you see a question…"* (meta-openers — start with the chemistry, not a meta-comment about the question type)

**Voice register:** simple, Hinglish-friendly English aimed at tier-2 North Indian JEE/NEET aspirants. Short, direct sentences. Conversational — teacher talking out loud, not writing a paper.

## ✏️ Formatting & Mechanical Rules

1. **No `###` or `##` headings.** The renderer leaves the `#` characters visible. Use **bold-icon lines** instead, on their own line, followed by a blank line then the body:
   - ✅ `**🧠 Why Iodide is the Best Nucleophile Here**`
   - ❌ `### 🧠 ...`
   - ❌ `**1. The "Aha!" Moment**` (generic template; always use a problem-specific name)
   - ❌ `🧠 **Title**` (icon outside bold — old format; switch to icon inside bold)

2. **Bespoke section titles.** Every 🧠 must have a *bespoke title* that names the specific concept/trick/trap. Compare:
   - ❌ `**🧠 The key insight**`
   - ❌ `**🧠 Use Le Chatelier's Principle**`
   - ✅ `**🧠 The Pyridine Trap — N Is Less Basic Than NH₃ Because the Lone Pair Is in $sp^2$**`
   - ✅ `**🧠 Hyperconjugation Beats Inductive Effect Here**`
   - ✅ `**🧠 Boiling Point Order Comes from H-Bonding, Not Molecular Weight**`

   The bespoke title is what the student remembers when they see the same trick on the next paper.

3. **No "Step 1 / Step 2" numbering. Skip obvious arithmetic.** Use short bolded inline cues only when the derivation genuinely pivots (`**Now balance the oxygen atoms:**`). Don't show every intermediate — JEE/NEET aspirants can balance a simple equation or compute moles in their head. Show conceptual moves; trust them on routine arithmetic.

4. **No bullet bloat.** Let derivations flow as prose. Bulleted lists only for genuinely defined enumerations (cases, oxidation-state assignments per atom in a polyatomic, comparison of 3+ compounds).

5. **Math/chemistry typography.** All math in `$...$`. Never `$$...$$` (centers the output, kills left-alignment). Always `\frac{}{}`, never `\dfrac{}{}`. End the solution with `$\boxed{\text{Answer: (Option) Value}}$`.

6. **Chemistry typography:**
   - **Inline formulas:** $\ce{H2SO4}$, $\ce{NH4+}$, $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$, $\ce{[Cu(NH3)4]^{2+}}$ — use `\ce{...}`.
   - **Coordination compounds with square brackets:** if `\ce{[...]}` renders incorrectly in your renderer, fall back to `\mathrm{[Cu(NH_3)_4]^{2+}}`.
   - **Reaction arrows:** $\ce{->}$, $\ce{<=>}$ inside `\ce{}`. Standalone: `$\rightarrow$`, `$\rightleftharpoons$`.
   - **Greek letters:** always inside `$...$`. Never bare `Δ`, `α` in prose.
   - **Units outside math:** plain text "mol/L", "kJ/mol", "g cm⁻³". Inside math: `\,\mathrm{mol/L}`.

7. **Length is set by substance, not by a target.** There is **no minimum and no maximum** word count. Calibrate to the actual teaching value of the question:

   - **Trivial questions** (single oxidation-state calculation, one-line algebra, direct recall): the 🧠 section should be **1–2 sentences naming the move**, the 🗺️ section just the arithmetic, ⚡ one real rule the student should memorise, ⚠️ *the* specific trap (singular). Total often ~150–250 words. **Do not pad.** Skip 🖼️ and 💡 unless they teach something.
   - **Medium questions** (concept recognition, multi-option comparison, identifying a reaction type): 🧠 sets up the diagnostic test in 2–4 sentences, 🗺️ walks each option briefly, ⚡ is the recognition rule, ⚠️ names the specific trap. Total often ~250–350 words.
   - **Hard questions** (multi-step calculations, non-obvious first move, JEE Advanced novelty, synthesis chains): full 🧠 setup with the "starting it right" framing is mandatory — this is where the workflow rule earns its keep. Length is whatever the chemistry requires.

   **The rule isn't "longer is better" or "shorter is better" — it is "match the explanation to the actual cognitive load of the question."** If a step is obvious, don't explain it. If the trap is unique to one option, say so in one sentence, not three. If a question genuinely needs 500 words, write 500 — but never inflate to hit a target, and never trim a load-bearing explanation just to look lean.

   **Signs of bloat to delete:** restating the question in your own words at the top of 🧠; explaining standard procedure ("we will compute the oxidation state") instead of doing it; multi-paragraph framing when the framing fits in one sentence; long ⚠️ sections listing every conceivable mistake instead of the one or two that actually catch students; ⚡ tips that just re-state the 🗺️ work.

   **Signs of under-explaining to fix:** skipping the bespoke 🧠 title on a question that has a genuine concept; merging 🗺️ steps when the conceptual transition between them is the teaching point; omitting the ⚠️ section entirely; not naming the underlying principle so the student can transfer it to a similar question next time.

8. **LaTeX in batch files.** Solutions live inside CommonJS template-literal strings (`scripts/chemistry-solutions/_batch_*.js`). Every `\` in LaTeX must be `\\`: `\\ce{H2SO4}`, `\\frac{a}{b}`, `\\boxed{...}`, `\\rightarrow`. Single backslash → silent corruption in the DB.

## 🧱 Solution Structure

Six sections. The first one (🧠) carries the most pedagogical weight — spend the most thought on it. The math/chemistry (🗺️) is the smaller part of the work; the thinking framework is the bigger part.

| # | Doc name | Icon | Required? | What this section does for the student |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Reading the Question | 🧠 | **Yes** | The teacher's most important job. Name the concept being tested, quote the visible clue from the question (a reagent, a structure, a phrase), and state the first concrete move. **Zero number-crunching, zero balanced equations.** This section often defines whether the student walks away able to solve similar problems on their own. |
| 2 | Visual Sketch | 🖼️ | **When there is a structure, diagram, or 3D geometry** | Two modes: (a) if the question **has a diagram** (image URL surfaced by `fetch-batch.js`), describe what it shows — the structural formula, the reaction scheme, the orbital overlap, the phase diagram. (b) If there is no diagram but a structure/geometry needs to be pictured, describe it in 1–2 sentences. Skip for pure numerical questions (mole calculations, sig figs, equivalent weight). |
| 3 | Working It Out | 🗺️ | **Yes** | The derivation/balancing/comparison, written as a teacher would walk through it at the board. Flowing prose. No numbered steps. Use conversational pivots like `**Now check the oxidation state of N:**` where the logic genuinely turns. Skip routine arithmetic — focus on the conceptual transitions and **why** each move comes next, not just **what** the move is. |
| 4 | The Smart Move | ⚡ | **Yes** | The shortcut the student should remember for **next time**. Must be a real **chemistry insight** (a periodic-trend rule, a resonance argument, a "look for the lone pair" check, a Markovnikov pattern, a sandmeyer trigger) — NOT "plug each option and check". The shortcut must work even if the question were converted to integer-input or subjective form. If no genuine shortcut exists, say so plainly: *"Honestly, no clever trick here — careful balancing is the fastest route."* |
| 5 | A Different Angle | 💡 | Optional (mandatory for retrosynthesis-style questions) | A second viable JEE/NEET-level approach — an alternate mechanism interpretation, a different reagent combination that gives the same product, a thermodynamic vs kinetic argument. Include only if it teaches the student something new about how to think — not just for completeness. **For synthesis questions, this is mandatory** — there is almost always a second valid route worth mentioning. |
| 6 | Where Students Get Stuck | ⚠️ | **Yes** | Diagnostic, not warning-label. Name the specific spot where students typically lose marks — sign error in redox balancing, wrong product in Markovnikov vs anti-Markovnikov, confusing $sp^2$ vs $sp^3$ lone pair, ortho vs para directing, $\ce{SN1}$ vs $\ce{SN2}$ leaving-group choice. Use teacher voice: *"If you ended up with the wrong product here, it is because..."*. One `⚠️` per warning line — don't double up with the heading icon. |

## 🖼️ Visual Sketch — Special Rules for Chemistry

Chemistry relies on structural diagrams more than math, and these come in two flavours:

**Mode A — Question already has a structural image.** The `fetch-batch.js` toolkit surfaces image URLs from `question_text.markdown`. Read the image first. The 🖼️ section then describes what the structure actually shows — the parent chain, the substituents, the stereochemistry, the reaction conditions written above the arrow.

> **CRITICAL — SVGs in this bank are filled white.** Same as the physics bank — the R2 question-bank SVGs use `fill="#fff"` on what they expect to be a dark background. Loading the URL in a browser shows a blank page — that does NOT mean the diagram is missing. Convert before reading:
> ```bash
> curl -s <url> -o /tmp/<display_id>.svg
> magick -background black -density 150 /tmp/<display_id>.svg /tmp/<display_id>.png
> # then Read /tmp/<display_id>.png
> ```
> Never skip the dark-background conversion for SVG files. PNG and JPG inputs can be read directly without conversion.

**Mode B — No image, but a structure helps.** Pure-numerical questions sometimes describe a compound in words. Give a 1–2 sentence "picture this" description so the student can sketch it on rough paper.

> Example: *"Picture a benzene ring with an -OH group at carbon 1 and -NO₂ at carbon 4 (para position). The -OH is an ortho/para director, but -NO₂ is meta-directing — the molecule's reactivity is set by these two competing groups."*

**Skip 🖼️ only when:** the question is pure numerical (mole balance, sig figs, equivalent weight), pure trend lookup (atomic radius down a group), or a pure conceptual statement check with no structure involved.

## 📐 Hand-Drawn Mechanism Markers — A Chemistry-Specific Tool

Some chemistry steps (especially arrow-pushing mechanisms with 5+ electron-flow arrows, complex aldol cyclisations, full SN1/SN2 transition states) are genuinely hard to convey in text alone. For these, **insert a marker in the solution** at the precise step where a hand-drawn diagram would help:

```markdown
The hydride from $\ce{LiAlH4}$ attacks the carbonyl carbon, and the
$\pi$ electrons of the C=O collapse onto oxygen, forming an alkoxide.

📐 [Solution diagram: arrow-pushing for the hydride attack — H⁻ → C, π
electrons → O, producing the tetrahedral alkoxide intermediate.]

Aqueous workup then protonates the alkoxide to give the alcohol.
```

The marker convention:
- Format: `📐 [Solution diagram: <description of what to draw>]` on its own line, between paragraphs.
- The description must be specific enough that someone else can draw the diagram from it.
- The toolkit `apply-batch.js` will detect these markers and auto-add entries to the Notion diagram-wishlist database under the question's chapter.
- The marker stays in the solution text; the student sees "diagram coming here" until you replace it with the actual generated image.

**When to use the marker:**
- ✅ Multi-arrow mechanism step where electron flow direction matters (SN2 transition state, aldol C-C bond formation, EAS arenium intermediate).
- ✅ Stereochemistry where wedge/dash bonds must be shown (E2 anti-periplanar geometry, Zaitsev vs Hofmann elimination).
- ✅ 3D conformation arguments (Newman projections, chair flip energetics).
- ❌ Simple structural formulas the student already has in the question.
- ❌ A "for clarity" picture that doesn't teach a step the prose missed.

If a single solution would benefit from 2 or more diagrams, that is acceptable — drop two markers at the appropriate steps.

## 🔎 Answer-Key Verification (before writing the solution)

Re-derive the answer independently. The stored answer is **not** authoritative — older ingestion passes left some stored answers null or wrong.

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Stored answer matches your derivation | Write solution; no `answer` field change |
| Stored answer is **null** | Fill it with your value |

| Question references a structural figure but no image URL is surfaced | Flag as blocking: `note: 'Structure referenced but not in question_text — cannot solve without seeing the compound.'` |

The boxed final line of the solution always shows **your** derived answer.

## 📖 Worked Example — How a Teacher Actually Talks (Chemistry)

The question (GOC-classic): *Arrange the following in increasing order of acidic strength: $\ce{HCOOH}$, $\ce{CH3COOH}$, $\ce{ClCH2COOH}$, $\ce{Cl3CCOOH}$.* Answer: $\ce{CH3COOH} < \ce{HCOOH} < \ce{ClCH2COOH} < \ce{Cl3CCOOH}$.

Here is how a teacher walks a student through it:

```markdown
**🧠 Acidity Comes Down to Conjugate-Base Stability — and Chlorine Stabilises by Induction**

Read this question slowly. The four acids look similar — they all have the $\ce{-COOH}$ group. So the question is really asking: which of their **conjugate bases** is the most stable? The more stable the base, the stronger the acid.

The deciding factor here is the **inductive effect** of the substituents. Chlorine is electron-withdrawing — it pulls electron density away from the $\ce{-COO^-}$ group, spreading out the negative charge and stabilising the base. Methyl, on the other hand, is electron-donating (weak hyperconjugation effect) — it pushes electron density toward the negative charge, destabilising the base.

So the order of acid strength will follow: (1) how many electron-withdrawing groups, and (2) how electron-donating any non-withdrawing groups are.

**🗺️ Working It Out**

Compare the four acids' substituents on the $\alpha$-carbon:

- $\ce{CH3COOH}$ — $\ce{CH3}$ group, weakly electron-donating. Destabilises the conjugate base. **Weakest acid.**
- $\ce{HCOOH}$ — just $\ce{H}$, neither donating nor withdrawing. Baseline.
- $\ce{ClCH2COOH}$ — one $\ce{Cl}$, electron-withdrawing. Stabilises the base.
- $\ce{Cl3CCOOH}$ — three $\ce{Cl}$ atoms. Maximum stabilisation. **Strongest acid.**

So the order is $\ce{CH3COOH} < \ce{HCOOH} < \ce{ClCH2COOH} < \ce{Cl3CCOOH}$.

**⚡ The Smart Move**

For any "acidity comparison" between $\ce{-COOH}$ compounds, just count and rank the electron-withdrawing groups on the $\alpha$-carbon. More Cl, F, $\ce{NO2}$, or $\ce{-CN}$ = stronger acid. Electron-donating groups (alkyl, $\ce{-NH2}$ in neutral state, $\ce{-OCH3}$ at $\alpha$) = weaker acid. One rule, applied four times — done in 30 seconds.

The same rule applied to halogen choice: $\ce{F > Cl > Br > I}$ as electron-withdrawers (because F is most electronegative). So $\ce{FCH2COOH} > \ce{ClCH2COOH} > \ce{BrCH2COOH}$.

**⚠️ Where Students Get Stuck**

Two main mistakes. **One:** ranking by the **acid** structure instead of the **conjugate base**. Some students reason "$\ce{CH3COOH}$ has more electrons, so it is a stronger acid". That is backwards — strength is set by base stability, not acid stability. Always switch to the conjugate base before reasoning. **Two:** confusing inductive (through-bond) effect with resonance (through-$\pi$) effect. Chlorine on a saturated carbon is purely inductive. Chlorine on a benzene ring has both inductive (withdrawing) and resonance (donating) effects, and the net result depends on position. Don't carry resonance arguments into saturated systems.

$\boxed{\text{Answer: } \ce{CH3COOH} < \ce{HCOOH} < \ce{ClCH2COOH} < \ce{Cl3CCOOH}}$
```

**What this solution does differently from a textbook:**

- The 🧠 section explains the *one underlying principle* (acidity = conjugate base stability), then names the specific tool (inductive effect). The actual ranking is short.
- The teacher uses direct address: *"Read this question slowly"*, *"The deciding factor here is..."*, *"Don't carry resonance arguments into saturated systems"*. The student feels someone is talking to them.
- The 🗺️ section walks through each compound briefly without listing every electron-pushing arrow.
- The ⚡ section gives a clear rule for next time and extends it to halogens — broader teaching.
- The ⚠️ section names two distinct common mistakes (wrong frame: acid vs base; wrong context: inductive vs resonance).
- The English is simple. No idioms, no fancy words, short sentences.

This is the bar. Every solution we write should leave the student thinking *"now I see how to start that kind of problem — I can do the next one myself"*.

## ✅ Pre-Submit Checklist

Before saving any solution:

1. Did I read the question's structural image (if it has one) before writing?
2. Did I re-derive the answer independently and compare with the stored answer?
3. Does my 🧠 open with a **bespoke title** naming the specific concept/trick?
4. Does my 🧠 open with chemistry — not a meta-comment about the question type?
5. Does the boxed answer match my derivation (and disagree with stored only if I checked twice)?
6. Is the length of my prose set by the substance of the question, not by a target — no padding for short questions, no premature trimming for genuinely complex ones?
7. Did I delete every adjective that was not load-bearing?
8. Is every `$` paired? Every `{` paired? No `$$`? No `\dfrac`?
9. Does the solution end with `$\boxed{\text{Answer: ...}}$`?
10. For mechanism-heavy questions, did I insert `📐 [Solution diagram: ...]` markers at the steps where arrow-pushing or stereochem would benefit from a picture?
11. Any "Wait, let me re-read" / "Standard problem with answer..." / "If we assume..." uncertainty leakage? If yes, re-solve and rewrite.
