# ⚛️ Physics Solution Workflow

This workflow mirrors `math-solution-workflow.md` line-for-line in structure and voice. Physics-specific rules (signs, vectors, $g$, units, diagrams) are layered on top — they do not replace the math philosophy, they extend it.

> **The main struggle for JEE/NEET students is figuring out HOW TO START a problem.** Once a student picks up the right thread, the algebra and unit-juggling usually follow. They get stuck at the *first move* — because they can't connect what the question shows them to a principle they already know.

So every solution we write has one job above all others: **teach the student how to start.** Show them how to read the question, what to notice first, what physical principle the examiner is pointing at, and how to translate that recognition into the first concrete move.

This isn't a textbook. It isn't an AI describing physics. It's a senior teacher standing beside a stuck student at the whiteboard, talking them through it.

---

## 🎯 The Starting-Move Principle (Read This First)

Every solution gives disproportionate care to **how to begin**. Four concrete demands:

1. **Name the principle in plain language.** "This is a momentum-conservation problem." "This is the apparent-weight-in-a-lift trick." "This is just $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ in disguise." "This is the work-energy theorem — forget Newton's laws here." Once a student has a category label, they can build a mental file: *"ah, that kind of problem"*. Next time they meet a similar one, the file opens automatically.

2. **Point at the visible clue.** Quote the specific phrase or number from the question that *should* have triggered the recognition. *"The phrase 'comes to rest' is the giveaway — final velocity is zero."* *"Smooth surface means no friction, so mechanical energy is conserved."* *"$g$ is given as 10, so use 10 throughout — do not switch to 9.8."* Students need to learn what to **read** in the question, not just what to compute.

3. **State the first concrete move.** Not "set up the equations" (vague), but "find the velocity at the lowest point using energy conservation — that's our anchor". The first move must be unambiguous and immediate.

4. **Defer the algebra.** The 🧠 section does **zero number-crunching**. It only sets up the thinking. Numbers come after the student has the framing.

If a student reads the 🧠 section and feels *"ah, now I see what to do"* — even before they read the rest — you've done your job. That's the bar.

## 🌱 Plain English — Even a Student Weak in English Must Follow the Physics

These solutions are read by tier-2 and tier-3 town students across India (Himachal, Uttarakhand, UP, Bihar, Punjab). Many are very good at physics but not strong in English. The physics must come through clearly even if the student does not understand every English word.

Two hard rules:

1. **Use the simplest English word that works.** Words like *see, look, notice, remember, easy, first step, main mistake, save time* are perfect. If a refined or literary word comes to mind, replace it with its everyday version. If you would not say it to a student in a coaching class, do not write it in the solution.

2. **No idioms, no poetic phrases.** English idioms confuse non-native readers. Standard physics vocabulary (momentum, impulse, escape velocity, free-fall, projectile, terminal velocity, normal force, friction, work-energy theorem, conservation, simple harmonic motion) is fine — these are JEE/NEET terms the student must know. What we are simplifying is the **English around the physics**, not the physics itself.

**Word-swap table — don't use the left, use the right:**

| Don't use | Use instead |
|---|---|
| operand, weakest input | number, smaller value |
| bottleneck | smallest, weakest |
| calibrated | set right, adjusted |
| diagnose, evaluate | check |
| deterministic | fixed |
| ambiguous | not clear, unclear |
| load-bearing | important |
| trivially, demonstrably, inherently | simply, clearly, naturally |
| running diagnostics, the diagnostic | the check |
| in textbook fashion | clearly, exactly |
| tucked, tucked inside | placed, drawn, sitting |
| in disguise, hidden in plain sight | the hint is here; let me point it out |
| burn time, burn 3 minutes | waste time, takes 3 minutes |
| drops out cleanly, collapses | comes out directly, becomes easy |
| slick (trick), elegant | quick, clean, simple |
| the giveaway, the gold | the key, the main hint |
| scan your eye, train your eye | look carefully, look for |
| reach for (figurative) | use, apply |
| land on (figurative) | becomes, equals, comes out to be |
| diagnostic spot | where students go wrong |
| crucial, paramount, leverage, elucidate | important, key, use, explain |

**Sentence length rule.** Target under 20 words per sentence; never over 30. If a sentence has more than one comma, or an em-dash, check whether it can be broken into two short sentences. Short sentences are easier to read for students whose first language is not English.

## 🗣 Voice — You Are a Teacher Standing Next to a Student

You are a senior JEE/NEET teacher writing for a student in your class. The student is bright but inexperienced. You're not lecturing from a podium — you're beside them at the board, talking out loud as you think through the problem with them.

**Tone patterns to USE:**

- *"Read the question slowly."*
- *"Look at this carefully. Notice that..."*
- *"Do you see something special?"*
- *"Whenever you see X in a problem, your first step should be Y."*
- *"This is the habit I want you to build."*
- *"First step:"*
- *"Many students mark this — but..."*
- *"Remember this. It comes in JEE/NEET almost every year."*
- *"Save time by..."*
- *"Be careful with signs."*
- *"Done. Now the next part."*
- *"Here is what I want you to remember for next time."*
- *"If you got stuck here, the main reason is..."*

**Tone patterns to AVOID:**

- *"It can be observed that..."* (textbook)
- *"We will now compute..."* (lecture-hall passive)
- *"Let's dive in", "Let's break this down", "In conclusion"* (AI cliché)
- *"Hence", "thus", "therefore"* in every other line (formal connective — okay once or twice)
- *"Crucial", "delineate", "paramount", "leverage", "elucidate"* (showy vocabulary)
- *"Tucked inside", "hidden in plain sight"* (literary)
- *"The giveaway", "the gold"* (idioms)
- *"Reach for", "scan your eye"* (figurative)
- *"Drops out cleanly", "collapses"* (figurative)
- *"Burn time", "burn 3 minutes"* — use *"takes 3 minutes"* or *"wastes time"*
- *"When you are asked to…"* / *"Whenever you see a question…"* (meta-openers — start with the physics, not a meta-comment about the question type)

**Voice register:** simple, Hinglish-friendly English aimed at tier-2 North Indian JEE/NEET aspirants. Short, direct sentences. Conversational — teacher talking out loud, not writing a paper.

## ✏️ Formatting & Mechanical Rules

1. **No `###` or `##` headings.** The renderer leaves the `#` characters visible. Use **bold-icon lines** instead, on their own line, followed by a blank line then the body:
   - ✅ `**🧠 The 90° Trick at Max Height**`
   - ❌ `### 🧠 ...`
   - ❌ `**1. The "Aha!" Moment**` (generic template; always use a problem-specific name)
   - ❌ `🧠 **Title**` (icon outside bold — old format; switch to icon inside bold)

2. **Bespoke section titles.** Every 🧠 must have a *bespoke title* that names the specific principle/trick/trap. Compare:
   - ❌ `**🧠 The key insight**`
   - ❌ `**🧠 Use $a = v\,dv/dx$**`
   - ✅ `**🧠 The 90° Trick at Max Height**`
   - ✅ `**🧠 Why the Kilogram Catch Matters Here**`
   - ✅ `**🧠 Stopping Distance Scales with $v^2$**`

   The bespoke title is what the student remembers when they see the same trick on the next paper.

3. **No "Step 1 / Step 2" numbering. Skip obvious arithmetic.** Use short bolded inline cues only when the derivation genuinely pivots (`**Now find the velocity at B:**`). Don't show every intermediate — JEE/NEET aspirants can expand a square or solve a 2×2 system in their head. Show conceptual moves; trust them on routine algebra.

4. **No bullet bloat.** Let derivations flow as prose. Bulleted lists only for genuinely defined enumerations (cases, statements in a Statement-I/II problem, distinct forces on a free-body).

5. **Math typography.** All math in `$...$`. Never `$$...$$` (centers the output, kills left-alignment). Always `\frac{}{}`, never `\dfrac{}{}`. End the solution with `$\boxed{\text{Answer: (Option) Value}}$`.

6. **Physics typography:**
   - **Vectors:** `$\vec{v}$`, `$\hat{i}$`, `$\hat{j}$`, `$\hat{k}$`. Components as `$v_x$, $v_y$, $v_z$`.
   - **Units inside math:** `\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}` not m/s. Outside math, plain text "m/s" is fine.
   - **Greek letters and arrows:** always inside `$...$`. Never bare `Δ`, `α`, `→` in prose.
   - **Sign convention:** state it once at the start of 🗺️ when it matters. *"Take upward as positive."* Then stick to it.
   - **$g$ value:** match what the question gave you. If $g = 10$, use 10 — never silently substitute 9.8.
   - **Unit consistency:** convert all quantities to SI at the start of 🗺️ if the question mixes km/h and m/s. Show the conversion once.

7. **Word target:** **300–400 words** of prose per solution (excluding LaTeX). Going 10–15% over is acceptable for genuinely complex multi-step problems (projectile + relative motion, two-body collision + energy). Going 30%+ over is a workflow violation — write tighter. Going much under 250 words usually means the 🧠 section is doing too little teaching.

8. **LaTeX in batch files.** Solutions live inside CommonJS template-literal strings (`scripts/physics-solutions/_batch_*.js`). Every `\` in LaTeX must be `\\`: `\\frac{a}{b}`, `\\boxed{...}`, `\\vec{v}`, `\\mathrm{m\\,s^{-1}}`. Single backslash → silent corruption in the DB.

## 🧱 Solution Structure

Six sections. The first one (🧠) carries the most pedagogical weight — spend the most thought on it. The math (🗺️) is the smaller part of the work; the thinking framework is the bigger part.

| # | Doc name | Icon | Required? | What this section does for the student |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Reading the Question | 🧠 | **Yes** | The teacher's most important job. Name the physical principle, quote the visible clue from the question, and state the first concrete move. **Zero number-crunching.** This section often defines whether the student walks away able to solve similar problems on their own. |
| 2 | Visual Sketch | 🖼️ | **When there's a diagram or when picturing helps** | Two modes: (a) if the question **has a diagram** (image URL surfaced by `fetch-batch.js`), describe what the diagram actually shows — the inclined plane, the pulley, the circuit, the wave pattern. (b) If there's no diagram but a sketch helps, give a 1–2 sentence verbal description of what to picture or quickly draw on rough paper. Skip only for purely algebraic/numerical questions where no picture is needed. |
| 3 | Working It Out | 🗺️ | **Yes** | The derivation, written as a teacher would walk through it at the board. Flowing prose. No numbered steps. State sign convention or $g$-value at the top if needed. Use conversational pivots like `**Now find the velocity at B:**` where the logic genuinely turns. Skip routine arithmetic — focus on the conceptual transitions and **why** each move comes next, not just **what** the move is. |
| 4 | The Smart Move | ⚡ | **Yes** | The shortcut the student should remember for **next time**. Must be a real **physics insight** (symmetry, conservation, dimensional analysis, limiting case, sign argument) — NOT "plug each option and check". The shortcut must work even if the question were converted to integer-input or subjective form. If no genuine shortcut exists, say so plainly: *"Honestly, no clever trick here — careful calculation is the fastest route."* |
| 5 | A Different Angle | 💡 | Optional | A second viable JEE/NEET-level method (e.g., energy method instead of force method, vector approach instead of component breakdown, dimensional analysis as a sanity check). Include only if it teaches the student something new about how to think — not just for completeness. |
| 6 | Where Students Get Stuck | ⚠️ | **Yes** | Diagnostic, not warning-label. Name the specific spot where students typically lose marks — sign error, km/h vs m/s, $g = 9.8$ vs 10, forgotten factor of 2, missed direction of friction, mass forgotten in momentum-vs-velocity confusion. Use teacher voice: *"If you ended up with the wrong sign here, it's because..."*. One `⚠️` per warning line — don't double up with the heading icon. |

## 🖼️ Visual Sketch — Special Rule for Physics

Physics relies on diagrams far more than math. The 🖼️ section in physics is **almost always required**, with two distinct modes depending on whether the question shipped with an image:

**Mode A — Question has an image (most common).** The `fetch-batch.js` toolkit surfaces image URLs from `question_text.markdown`. Read the image first, then describe in 🖼️ what the diagram actually shows — block on incline, masses on pulley, circuit with two cells, wave with marked points, etc. This grounds the student in the same picture you're solving from.

> **CRITICAL — SVGs in this bank are filled white.** The R2 question-bank SVGs use `fill="#fff"` (white strokes/text on what they expect to be a dark background). Loading the URL directly in a browser shows a blank page — that does NOT mean the diagram is missing. Convert before reading:
> ```bash
> curl -s <url> -o /tmp/<display_id>.svg
> magick -background black -density 150 /tmp/<display_id>.svg /tmp/<display_id>.png
> # then Read /tmp/<display_id>.png — the diagram renders perfectly
> ```
> Never skip the dark-background conversion for SVG files — the raw SVG XML is parseable text but unreadable to a multimodal model; the white-on-black PNG is what makes the figure legible. PNG and JPG inputs can be read directly without conversion.

> Example: *"The diagram shows a $2$ kg block on a $30°$ incline, connected by a string over a pulley at the top to a $3$ kg block hanging vertically. The incline is rough — the surface marking shows friction is present."*

**Mode B — No image, but a picture helps.** Pure-numerical questions sometimes describe a setup in words. Give a 1–2 sentence "picture this" description so the student can draw it on rough paper.

> Example: *"Picture two trains on parallel tracks, one moving east at $60$ km/h, the other west at $40$ km/h, with a bird flying back and forth between them."*

**Skip 🖼️ only when:** the question is pure formula manipulation (e.g., "find dimensions of $G$"), pure dimensional analysis, or a multiple-statement conceptual check with no setup. If in doubt, include it.

## 🔎 Answer-Key Verification (before writing the solution)

Re-derive the answer independently. The stored answer is **not** authoritative — older ingestion passes left some stored answers null or wrong.

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Stored answer matches your derivation | Write solution; no `answer` field change |
| Stored answer is **null** | Fill it with your value |
| Stored answer **disagrees** (after re-checking twice) | Overwrite with your value; the apply-batch log shows the change |
| Your derivation matches **no option** | Do NOT invent an option. Flag the question via `force_flag: { severity: 'blocking', note: '...' }`. Never hand-wave to the stored option. |
| Question references a figure but no image URL is surfaced | Flag as blocking: `note: 'Image referenced but not in question_text — cannot solve without seeing the diagram.'` |

The boxed final line of the solution always shows **your** derived answer.

## 📝 Question-Text Sanity Check

Parse the question first. If you spot an OCR-style typo (`= 10` where `= 0` was meant, missing brace, two operators adjacent, `9` for `g`, missing unit) and the intended fix is **unambiguous given the options**, silently repair `question_text.markdown` via `question_text_fix: { from: '...', to: '...' }` and log it. If the fix is ambiguous, flag instead.

## 📖 Worked Example — How a Teacher Actually Talks (Physics)

The question (NLM-classic): *A $2$ kg block is pushed against a vertical wall by a horizontal force $F$. The coefficient of friction between block and wall is $0.5$. Find the minimum $F$ to keep the block from sliding down. ($g = 10\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$).* Answer: $40$ N.

Here's how a teacher walks a student through it:

```markdown
**🧠 Friction on a Vertical Wall — Normal Comes from the Push, Not Gravity**

Read this question carefully. The block is against a *vertical* wall, not on a horizontal surface. That changes everything. On a table, the normal force comes from gravity. On a wall, the normal force comes from whatever is pushing the block into the wall — here, the force $F$.

So the friction available to hold the block up is $\mu F$, not $\mu mg$. This is the one insight that solves the problem. The minimum $F$ is the one where friction just barely equals the weight pulling the block down.

This is the habit I want you to build: whenever a block is on a *vertical* surface, ask "what is providing the normal force?" before doing anything else.

**🖼️ Visual Sketch**

Picture the block pressed against a vertical wall. The horizontal force $F$ pushes the block into the wall from the right. Weight $mg$ pulls the block down. The wall pushes back with normal force $N = F$. Friction $f$ on the block acts upward (opposing the tendency to slide down).

**🗺️ Working It Out**

Take upward as positive. The block is in equilibrium, so all forces balance.

Horizontal: $N = F$ (the wall pushes back with whatever $F$ pushes it with).

Vertical: friction $f$ holds the block against gravity. At the minimum $F$, friction is at its maximum value: $f_\text{max} = \mu N = \mu F$.

For the block to just barely not slide: $\mu F = mg$.

So $F = \frac{mg}{\mu} = \frac{2 \times 10}{0.5} = 40\,\mathrm{N}$.

**⚡ The Smart Move**

Whenever a block sits on a vertical surface and is held up by friction, remember this one line: $F_\text{min} = \frac{mg}{\mu}$. No free-body diagram needed once you've seen the pattern. This appears in JEE Main almost every other year — sometimes with the wall, sometimes with two opposing walls (twice the friction). One identification, one formula, done.

**⚠️ Where Students Get Stuck**

Two main mistakes. **One:** writing $f = \mu mg$ out of habit. That's the formula for a block on a horizontal surface where gravity supplies the normal force. On a wall, normal force comes from $F$, so $f = \mu F$. Use the wrong one and your answer is off by a factor of $\mu^2$. **Two:** forgetting that at *minimum* $F$, friction is at its *maximum* value. Many students write $f = \mu N$ as if friction is always at the limit. It's only at the limit when the block is about to slide — which is exactly the minimum-$F$ condition. If $F$ is larger, friction is less than $\mu N$, and the block still doesn't slide. The wording "minimum $F$ to keep from sliding" is the clue.

$\boxed{\text{Answer: (a) } 40\,\mathrm{N}}$
```

**What this solution does differently from a textbook:**

- The 🧠 section spends most of its space on the *one physical insight* (normal force comes from $F$, not from $mg$). The actual math derivation is short.
- The teacher uses direct address: *"Read this carefully"*, *"This is the habit I want you to build"*, *"Remember this"*. The student feels someone is talking to them.
- The 🖼️ section makes the picture concrete before the math starts.
- The 🗺️ section explains *why* each move comes next, not only what.
- The ⚡ section gives a clear lesson for next time, not just a quick trick.
- The ⚠️ section names the exact place students go wrong, and closes the loop with the wording clue from the question.
- The English is simple. No idioms, no fancy words, short sentences. A student weak in English can still follow the physics.

This is the bar. Every solution we write should leave the student thinking *"now I see how to start that kind of problem — I can do the next one myself"*.

## ✅ Pre-Submit Checklist

Before saving any solution:

1. Did I read the question's image (if it has one) before writing?
2. Did I re-derive the answer independently and compare with the stored answer?
3. Does my 🧠 open with a **bespoke title** naming the specific principle/trick?
4. Does my 🧠 open with physics — not a meta-comment about the question type?
5. Does the boxed answer match my derivation (and disagree with stored only if I checked twice)?
6. Is my prose within ~300–400 words (excluding LaTeX)?
7. Did I delete every adjective that wasn't load-bearing?
8. Is every `$` paired? Every `{` paired? No `$$`? No `\dfrac`?
9. Does the solution end with `$\boxed{\text{Answer: ...}}$`?
10. Any "Wait, let me re-read" / "Standard problem with answer..." / "If we assume..." uncertainty leakage? If yes, re-solve and rewrite.
