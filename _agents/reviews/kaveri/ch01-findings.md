# Chapter 1 review — How I Taught My Grandmother to Read (+ poem "Bharat Our Land")

SUMMARY: This is a strong, largely faithful adaptation. Every `narrated_passage` of the Sudha Murty story and the Bharati poem I checked is reproduced **verbatim** from the NCERT PDF — the cardinal sin (paraphrasing the source as if it were the text) is not committed. The pedagogical scaffolding is genuinely good: glosses are mostly accurate, the comprehension questions reward inference, and the worked-examples model real exam answers. The single biggest systemic weakness is **fabricated/unverifiable biographical detail in the author-bio cards** (the "fun_fact," "cultural_context_card," "india_voice," and "meet_a_scientist" blocks) — Triveni's age at death is wrong, and several causal claims about who-inspired-whom are presented as fact when they are at best inference. A secondary systemic issue is **cross-page formulaic uniformity**: every one of the 17 pages runs the identical beat (banner image → intro text → content block → "reveal"-style reasoning_prompt → inline_quiz of 3 questions at difficulty 1/2/3), and the show-don't-tell / "specifics travel; generalisations don't" thesis is recycled almost word-for-word across Pages 5, 6, 9, and 15. A handful of quiz items have a defensible second answer or a giveaway.

TOP 5 MOST IMPORTANT FINDINGS (ranked):
1. [CRITICAL] Triveni's age at death is stated as **35** on three pages; she was born 1 Sept 1928 and died 29 July 1963 — i.e. **34**, six weeks short of 35. (Pages 1, 2, 10.)
2. [CRITICAL] Page 10 `callout/india_voice` asserts as fact that "Sudha Murty has said in interviews that Triveni's voice was the one that taught **her own grandmother** to ask for the alphabet." This is not in the source and is a fabricated/unverifiable biographical claim that also contradicts the story's own logic (it was the *granddaughter* reading Triveni aloud that moved Krishtakka, not "Triveni's voice").
3. [MAJOR] Page 8 `dialogue_role_play` debate frames are **mis-bucketed and duplicated**: "There's no doubt that…" appears under BOTH "for" and "against," and "There are two sides to this topic…" and "If I could make a point here…" are listed twice within the "against" column. The NCERT table assigns these to specific sides.
4. [MAJOR] Page 7 `apply_express` invents a binomial — "**high and low**" — and a verb-form ("dispelled") that are **not from this chapter's NCERT exercise**, while presenting the whole block as "Krishtakka *un*learned her shame," a claim the text never supports (she was never ashamed of her family; she was embarrassed to ask outsiders).
5. [MAJOR] Cross-page formulaic uniformity + recycled thesis: the "show, don't tell / specifics travel" point is delivered as the climactic reasoning_prompt or quiz answer on Pages 5, 6, 9, and 15, making the back half of the chapter read machine-stamped.

## A. Source fidelity

The narrated passages are faithful — this is the chapter's strongest dimension. Spot-checks of every `narrated_passage` block (Pages 2, 3, 12) against the PDF found the story and poem text reproduced word-for-word, including punctuation and the "..." trailing marks. No silent simplification of the original. Good.

[CRITICAL] A. unit-1-reflect-and-respond-grandmother › block#2/callout — Triveni's age at death is wrong. — EVIDENCE: "She died young, at 35." (also Page 2 cultural_context_card: "She died at 35"; Page 1 fun_fact again "died young, at 35"). Anasuya Shankar was born 1 Sept 1928 and died 29 July 1963 — she was **34**. — FIX: change all three to "died at 34" (or "died in 1963, not yet 35").

[CRITICAL] A. unit-1-about-sudha-murty › block#3/callout (india_voice) — Fabricated/unverifiable biographical claim presented as fact, and it contradicts the story's own causal chain. — EVIDENCE: "Sudha Murty has said in interviews that Triveni's voice was the one that taught **her own grandmother** to ask for the alphabet." — FIX: delete the sentence, or reduce to a hedged literary observation ("the novel Krishtakka loved is what gave her the words to want literacy") that the *text itself* supports.

[MAJOR] A. unit-1-story-part-1 › block#3/cultural_context_card — Over-precise death cause stated as established fact. — EVIDENCE: Page 10 india_voice: "She died of complications in childbirth at the age of 35." (Her death was a pulmonary embolism ten days after childbirth — "complications in childbirth" is loosely defensible, but paired with the wrong age it compounds the error.) — FIX: "She died in 1963, shortly after childbirth, at 34."

[MAJOR] A. unit-1-about-subramania-bharati › block#4/callout (threads_of_curiosity) — Timeline compressed inaccurately. — EVIDENCE: "the elephant … knocked him down and stepped on him … The internal damage contributed to his death **three months later**, at the age of 39." The Triplicane incident was in 1920 and Bharati died 11 Sept 1921 — roughly ten months later, not three. — FIX: "contributed to his death the following year" or "less than a year later."

[MINOR] A. unit-1-about-subramania-bharati › block#2/meet_a_scientist — The Chellamma "kept his poems alive by reciting them from memory" claim is repeated as the difficulty-3 quiz answer (block#5). It is a commonly-told account but not solidly documented; flagging as low-confidence, not a hard error. — EVIDENCE: "Much of what we have today exists because she remembered." — FIX: soften to "is said to have" if it cannot be sourced.

[MINOR] A. unit-1-about-sudha-murty › block#2/meet_a_scientist — The block type is literally named "meet_a_scientist" for an author bio. Sudha Murty trained as an engineer, so it is not absurd, but the framing ("scientist") is off for a literature chapter. Cosmetic/schema mismatch, not a content error. — FIX: none needed if the renderer relabels; otherwise note the mismatch.

## B. Pedagogical depth

Mostly strong. Glosses are concrete, Hindi equivalents are accurate (परिवहन व्यवस्था, धारावाहिक, पुण्य, etc.), and the reasoning_prompts on Pages 2, 3, 4, 5 do real interpretive work (the "identify with the protagonist" prompt and the "why does she laugh" prompt are genuinely good). Two real weaknesses:

[MAJOR] B. unit-1-vocabulary-binomials-and-prefixes › block#7/apply_express — A grammar challenge teaches a usage rule on a word the chapter never introduces, and frames it on a claim the text contradicts. — EVIDENCE: intro: "Krishtakka *un*learned her shame and *re*started her education." Krishtakka is never shown as ashamed of herself or her family — she was *embarrassed to ask outsiders*, which is different; the framing mis-states her motive. The same block introduces "high and low" (challenge ch1-gram-04) and "dispelled" (ch1-gram-02), neither of which is from Kaveri's binomial/prefix exercise. — FIX: rewrite the intro to match the text ("Krishtakka set aside her embarrassment"); restrict examples to the chapter's own list.

[MINOR] B. unit-1-vocabulary-binomials-and-prefixes › block#5/vocabulary_lab self_check (b0e7a7bc) — Subtly misleading gloss. — EVIDENCE: explanation says "*unfortunate* is the adjective form; the noun is *misfortune*." This implies *misfortune* is the only noun and pairs it as the "opposite of fortune," but the cleaner opposite-noun of *fortune* in the negative-prefix exercise sense is debatable (mis- = bad, not strictly "not"). Defensible but muddier than the surrounding cards. — FIX: tighten to "mis- = bad/wrong → misfortune = bad luck."

[MINOR] B. unit-1-poem-vocabulary-in-context › block#6/vocabulary_lab (5847e69b) — Invented/awkward word forms offered as examples. — EVIDENCE: "Other -ity nouns: *purity, generosity, sacredness/sacredity, divinity, eternity*." — "sacredity" is not a word (the noun is *sanctity*/*sacredness*); the memory_hook then repeats "Sacred → sacrality" — also non-standard. — FIX: drop "sacredity"/"sacrality"; use *sanctity*.

[MINOR] B. unit-1-poem-vocabulary-in-context › block#6 (ab74b345) — EVIDENCE: "Other -ous adjectives in the poem: *auspicious, divinous (root of divinest)*." "Divinous" is not a word; the root is *divine*. — FIX: remove "divinous."

## C. Assessment integrity

Generally solid: distractors are usually real misconceptions and explanations cite the text. But several items have problems.

[MAJOR] C. unit-1-reflect-and-respond-grandmother › block#5/inline_quiz (009cbc3e) — Distractor C is **defensible as correct**, so a thinking student is marked wrong. — EVIDENCE: stem "Why might Anasuya Shankar have chosen a male-sounding pen-name?"; keyed answer A ("Publishers of that era took male writers more seriously"); distractor C ("Her own family disapproved, so she hid behind a false name"). Family disapproval of women writing was a real and common reason in that era, and the page's own text doesn't exclude it. The item is keyed to the page's stated reason, but C is not a *false* proposition, only an *unstated* one. — FIX: change C to something the page actively rules out, or reword the stem to "According to this page, why…".

[MAJOR] C. unit-1-themes-grandmother-story › block#3/callout (voices_that_inspire) — Mis-rendered glyph breaks the reflection question. — EVIDENCE: the block ends with "?? Where else have you seen this idea…" — the "??" is a corrupted emoji/marker. Also the same block embeds Tagore's "Where the mind is without fear" as a tangential second author; it is solid context but adds load on a theme page already carrying three themes + two callouts. — FIX: repair the glyph; consider trimming the Tagore callout to keep the page on-topic.

[MAJOR] C. unit-1-debate-can-students-promote-literacy › block#3/dialogue_role_play — Frames duplicated and mis-bucketed vs. the NCERT table. — EVIDENCE: "There's no doubt that…" appears in BOTH the "for" and "against" arrays; "There are two sides to this topic…" and "If I could make a point here…" each appear **twice** inside "against". The NCERT 'For'/'Against' table assigns each prompt to one column. — FIX: de-duplicate and re-map to the source table (e.g. "On the contrary," "There are two sides," "If I could make a point" belong to Against; "In addition to that," "I strongly feel," "I have a reason to believe" to For).

[MINOR] C. unit-1-poem-vocabulary-in-context › block#7/inline_quiz (fb6bf271) — Giveaway in the explanation/stem pairing. — EVIDENCE: stem names "two words … 'full of [that root]'"; only one option lists two -ous adjectives ("generous and auspicious"), so the answer is findable purely by spotting "two -ous words" without understanding the suffix. — FIX: make at least one distractor also a plausible same-suffix pair.

[MINOR] C. unit-1-working-with-text-grandmother › block#11/reasoning_prompt (1be1a68b) — "No single correct answer" framing is fine, but the reveal then ranks three "defensible picks" so exhaustively that it converts an open prompt into a closed checklist — the student's own pick is implicitly graded against the three. — FIX: present the three as *examples*, not the full set.

[MINOR] C. ## E. Literary & linguistic accuracy

The literary-device labelling is mostly correct and is one of the chapter's better features (the personification/metaphor/hyperbole calls on Page 12 are right). Issues:

[MAJOR] E. unit-1-poem-themes-and-features › block#7/worked_example (Hyphens in Stanza 1) — Conflates two different marks and mislabels the source. — EVIDENCE: title says "the use of **hyphens (em dashes)**." The NCERT exercise asks about "hyphens"; the poem as printed in the PDF uses a plain hyphen "-", while this corpus renders them as em dashes "—" in the narrated_passage. Calling them "hyphens (em dashes)" treats two distinct typographic marks as synonyms, which is exactly the kind of precision an English chapter should model. — FIX: pick one term; if the rendered poem uses "—", call them dashes and note the original printed a hyphen.

[MINOR] E. unit-1-poem-bharat-our-land › block#4/literary_devices_highlighter (device: rhyme) — Mislabelled device. — EVIDENCE: device is tagged "rhyme" but the explanation describes **alliteration** ("*peerless* and *praise* alliterate with the *p* sound") and consonance, not rhyme. — FIX: retag the device "alliteration."

[MINOR] E. unit-1-poem-themes-and-features › block#4/worked_example (Mood & Tone & Rhyme) — Internal tension on rhyme scheme. — EVIDENCE: "Rhyme scheme: largely unrhymed (free verse)." Defensible for this translation, but the same page's tone_meter and device blocks lean on "chant-like rhythm"; calling a chant-like patriotic ode "free verse" without noting it is a *translation* (the Tamil original is metrical) slightly misleads. — FIX: add "in this English translation" qualifier.

[MINOR] E. unit-1-poem-vocabulary-in-context › block#6 (0ebbac75) — Pronunciation error. — EVIDENCE: "divinest … pronunciation: di-VINE-ist."
