# Chapter 5 review — "The World of Limitless Possibilities" (Paralympics)

SUMMARY: This is a strong, source-faithful build: the Dr. Deepa Malik interview and the David Roth poem "Nine Gold Medals" are quoted verbatim, the vocabulary scaffolding is dense and genuinely glossed, and the reasoning prompts do real interpretive work rather than recall. The most serious problems are at the quiz level, not the prose level: several distractors are defensible-as-correct or ambiguous (most damagingly a fact-vs-opinion item whose "two facts" distractor is arguably right, and a "biggest challenge" item the text partly contradicts), and one worked-example answer (ch5-WE on page 6) contains a self-contradicting parenthetical the author left in the published field. The single biggest systemic weakness is **assessment integrity in the fact/opinion and "single correct interpretation" items**, where the chapter's own confident explanations paper over genuine ambiguity that a thinking student would be marked wrong on. Source fidelity is otherwise clean except for one externally-true-but-unsourced fabrication risk on page 9 (Murlikant Petkar "first gold").

FULL FINDINGS BY DIMENSION

## A. Source fidelity
Largely clean. The interview text on pages 2–4 (narrated_passage blocks) matches the PDF verbatim sentence-by-sentence (e.g. "I had two choices—squander my life in remorse or transform it to a world of limitless possibilities"; "every setback is an opportunity to prove your strength… disability is not a limitation; it's a unique strength waiting to be unleashed"). The poem on page 11 matches David Roth's text as printed.

[MINOR · A] Page 11 narrated_passage (block #2) renders stanza 7 as "a banner above that said **'Special Olympics'**" — the PDF prints it as "said **(Special Olympics)**" with parentheses. Cosmetic, but it is a silent normalization of the source punctuation. — FIX: match the source's parentheses or note the change.

[MINOR · A] Page 1 fun_fact callout (block #2) states Dr. Malik "became India's first-ever woman to win a Paralympic medal (silver in shot-put, Rio 2016)." This is correct and supported by page 140 of the source ("India's first ever female Paralympics medallist across any sport"), but it is asserted on the *opening* page before the interview establishes it — front-loading a fact the source delivers later. Acceptable, noted for completeness.

[MAJOR · A/E] Page 9 `india_voice` (block #4): "Murlikant Petkar (India's first Paralympic **gold**, swimming, 1972)". The source (PDF p.138/154) says only "the first medal was clinched in swimming in 1972" — no "gold," no name. EVIDENCE: PDF "Bharat's debut in the Paralympics dates back to 1968, and the first medal was clinched in swimming in 1972." vs corpus "**Murlikant Petkar** (India's first Paralympic gold, swimming, 1972)". — FIX: either source the Petkar/gold claim explicitly as outside-NCERT enrichment, or downgrade to "India's first Paralympic medal (swimming, 1972)" to stay inside the source.

## B. Pedagogical depth & scaffolding
Genuinely strong. Glosses are example-rich and pitched at the right register (dengue, tabla, school bus). Reasoning prompts on pages 2, 3, 5, 12, 13 do real interpretive work (the "circumstances vs response" gap on page 2; "why is prejudice harder than disability" on page 3) rather than recall.

[MAJOR · B] Page 10 vocabulary_lab (block #4) defines `compassion` as "empathy PLUS the will to act and help." This is a *taught* definition, not a dictionary one — standard usage treats compassion as deep sympathy/concern, and "empathy + action" is a particular pedagogical model. The chapter then *grades on it* (page 10 self_check q "ec67427b…" marks "the will to ACT and help — empathy plus action" correct; page 13 theme_explorer and ch5-ae-08 reinforce it). A student who knows the ordinary meaning of compassion (feeling, not necessarily action) is penalised. — FIX: frame it as "in this chapter we'll use compassion to mean empathy + action" rather than presenting it as the word's definition.

[MINOR · B] Page 1 vocabulary_lab front-loads 8 fairly heavy words (resilience, quell, squander, remorse, indelible, fortitude, accolades, inclusivity) before the reading. The source itself only side-glosses these as they appear in context; pre-loading all 8 as flashcards risks decontextualised cramming. The in-passage glosses on pages 2–4 do this better. Mild redundancy rather than error.

[MINOR · B] Page 7 modal-auxiliary text (block #5) introduces "backshift the tense" (block #8) — "backshift" is jargon used without a plain-English unpack on first appearance for a second-language reader. The example shows it, but the term lands undefined. — FIX: gloss "backshift = move the verb one tense into the past."

## C. Assessment integrity
This is the weakest dimension.

[CRITICAL · C] Page 6 worked_example (block #6, id 5cbbf960… — "Imagery, alliteration & tone", solution string): the published solution contains an unedited authoring aside: "*'stumbled and staggered'* and *'dashed in the dirt'* (also acceptable: 'beaming faces' — **wait, not alliterative**; use 'gold… games', 'finish line')." EVIDENCE: that exact parenthetical is in the student-facing `solution` field. It is a thinking-out-loud artifact, and its replacement examples are wrong: "finish line" is not alliteration, and "gold… games" is a weak non-adjacent pairing. — FIX: delete the parenthetical; give clean alliteration only ("stumbled and staggered", "dashed in the dirt").

[MAJOR · C] Page 6 comprehension_checkpoint (block #4, q "bc56106d…") and Page 6 inline_quiz (block #9, q "d87d1a20…"): both test the same fact-vs-opinion pair and mark "two facts" as a *wrong* distractor. But "I feel it was a moment of personal victory" reports a *fact about her internal state* — a defensible reading is that both sentences state facts (one external, one introspective). The item treats the fact/opinion boundary as crisp when it is exactly the kind of edge the NCERT itself uses to provoke discussion ("Discuss your answers with your classmates and teacher"). EVIDENCE: explanation "'I feel it was a personal victory' is an OPINION (a belief)." — FIX: either pick a cleaner opinion sentence, or accept the ambiguity in the explanation rather than marking a reasonable student wrong.

[MAJOR · C] Page 3 inline_quiz (block #5, q "05958838-bbb7…"): stem "What was Dr. Malik's **biggest** challenge?" keys "Society's low expectations" and marks "Her physical paralysis after the spinal surgery" wrong. The source says "**One of the** significant challenges for me was societal perceptions" — not "the biggest." A student who reads paralysis (the originating, life-altering event) as the biggest challenge has a textually defensible answer. EVIDENCE: source "One of the significant challenges for me was societal perceptions." — FIX: change stem to "Which challenge does Dr. Malik single out as needing *mental* resilience?" so the keyed answer is the only fit.

[MAJOR · C] Page 5 theme_explorer (block #2) "Every setback is an opportunity" lists evidence "'I had two choices — squander my life in remorse or transform it'." Then Page 6 inline_quiz q "1c33dc08…", Page 13, and ch5-pr-25 repeatedly re-test the same "lesson is for everyone because it's about response not circumstance" point with near-identical stems and the "because it's about how we meet difficulty" answer. The repeated correct option is consistently the abstract/reframing one; a test-wise student can pattern-match to "the philosophical-sounding option" without reading. — FIX: vary the answer position and the cognitive demand; some of these should have the concrete option be correct.

[MAJOR · C] Page 6 inline_quiz (block #9, q "bf09f234…") analogy "ability : potential :: preconceived notions : ___" keys "stereotypes." This is fine, but the worked_example feeding it (block #6 "Extract 2") states the pairs are "near-synonyms" — ability and potential are *not* strict synonyms (potential = latent/future capacity; ability = present skill), so the analogy is taught on a slightly false premise. EVIDENCE: "Ability and potential are near-synonyms." — FIX: describe the relation as "closely related abstract nouns" rather than "near-synonyms."

[MINOR · C] Page 2 inline_quiz q "cd42b8c4…" stem quotes 'comfortably' ("How was Dr. Malik able to switch to para-athletics 'comfortably'?") but the source word is "comfortably"? — the PDF text reads "I decided to switch to para-athletics" with no "comfortably"; the NCERT *exercise* (Critical Reflection) uses "quite comfortably." ## D. AI-slop / authenticity
The prose mostly avoids the banned tells; no "delve/realm/tapestry/vibrant," em-dashes are within reason, and the closers are not patronising. But the structural uniformity is real.
