# Chapter 8 review — Follow That Dream (+ poem "Believe in Yourself")

SUMMARY: The chapter is, on its surface, careful and faithful — the narrated letter and poem reproduce Irene Chua's and Robert Langley's exact words, the vocabulary load is well-controlled, and the assessment volume is generous (26-question adaptive set + 18 apply-express). But it carries two genuine, student-invisible defects. (1) The reading silently DROPS a sentence of the original letter ("To fulfil them you will need to negotiate a path through a maze of hurdles. The dream will take a much longer time to realise, and the people who are participants in your dreamscape would be many more.") — and then later pages quiz students on that very dropped text and define "dreamscape" from it, so the book tests and glosses words the reader was never shown. (2) One practice item (ch8-pr-09) is internally broken: its stem quotes the "prospect of success" sentence but asks about "buoyed up," so the quoted evidence does not support the question. The single biggest systemic weakness is **cross-page formulaic uniformity** — every one of the 16 content pages runs the identical beat (banner image → 1-line framing text → reading/interactive block → reasoning_prompt with a long "reveal" → 3-question inline_quiz), and the reasoning "reveals" share a recurring AI cadence ("It's something far more hopeful than failure", "the honest answer is both yes and no") that reads machine-stamped across the chapter.

TOP 5 MOST IMPORTANT FINDINGS (ranked):
1. [CRITICAL] Source text dropped from the reading then quizzed later — the "maze of hurdles / dreamscape" sentence is omitted from the narrated passage (Page 3) but is the answer to a worked-example sub-question (Page 5) and the source of the "dreamscape" definition (Page 6). Students are tested on words they never read.
2. [CRITICAL] Broken practice item ch8-pr-09 (Page 17): stem quotes "When stamina is running out, the prospect of success will keep you on track" but then asks the meaning of "buoyed up" — the quoted sentence contains no such phrase, so the evidence contradicts the question.
3. [MAJOR] The factual claim that the ten-year/"world-class in ten years" idea is "supported by real research" (Page 1 callout) is stated as established science. The original letter makes no such research claim; this is an authored embellishment presented as fact to second-language students.
4. [MAJOR] Cross-page formulaic uniformity: 16 content pages on the identical structural template, and the long reasoning_prompt "reveals" share a repeated rhetorical mould (rhetorical-question opener → "both yes and no"/"something far more" reframe → triple-noun summary). The chapter passes page-by-page but reads machine-stamped end to end.
5. [MAJOR] AI-tell density in the authored commentary — "not/isn't X but Y" framing, reveal framing ("the truth is"/"the secret"), and patronising closers ("That is a beautiful note for a whole book to end on", "Wisdom without courage never begins") recur across nearly every reasoning_prompt and callout.

## B. Pedagogical depth
[MAJOR] unit-8-reflect-and-respond-follow-that-dream › block#2/callout — The "ten-year rule" is presented as proven science with no source, to readers who cannot check it. EVIDENCE: "This idea — that mastery takes roughly a decade of dedicated effort — is supported by real research into how the world's best athletes, musicians, scientists, and artists are made. There are no overnight champions." The letter itself asserts the ten-year figure as the mother's own claim, not as cited research. — FIX: attribute it as the letter's claim ("the mother states…"), or drop the unsourced "supported by real research" sentence.

[MINOR] unit-8-reading-part-1-then-plunge › block#5/reasoning_prompt — The prompt's premise mis-frames the usual advice to set up its own answer. EVIDENCE: "Isn't that the opposite of what we usually tell dreamers ('just go for it, don't think about the obstacles')?" presents a strawman ("we usually tell dreamers… don't think about the obstacles") so the reveal can knock it down. The reasoning is real but is scaffolded on a caricature. — FIX: keep the question but drop the loaded "what we usually tell dreamers" framing.

Otherwise pedagogically sound: vocabulary load stays at or below ~1 unfamiliar word per sentence; glosses (differentiates, singularly, conviction, imperative, plunge, buoyed up, prospect, a host of, siblings, wishful thinking, coursing through your veins, aspirations) are accurate and non-redundant; concepts are introduced before they are tested (first conditional and "could" jobs are taught on Page 7 before the Page 17 drill).

## C. Assessment integrity
[CRITICAL] ch8-practice-mastery › block#2/chapter_practice (ch8-pr-09) — Stem quotes one sentence but asks about a phrase from a different sentence; the quoted evidence does not contain the word being tested. EVIDENCE: question: "'When stamina is running out, the prospect of success will keep you on track.' Here, 'buoyed up' — used earlier in the same letter — most nearly means —". The quoted sentence has nothing to do with "buoyed up"; the awkward "used earlier in the same letter" patch confirms the mismatch. — FIX: re-quote the actual "buoyed up" sentence ("often you are buoyed up only by the knowledge that you are doing what you love best") in the stem.

[MINOR] unit-8-themes-follow-that-dream › block#5/inline_quiz (d91d6fb4 / d9af3a18-style items, all pages) — Recurring pattern: in many inline-quiz items the correct option is the only *plainly-worded* one while the three distractors are written in a longer, slightly-too-elaborate "obviously-wrong" register ("That sincere, heartfelt wishing is really all that it takes", "That chasing after dreams is rarely worth all the trouble"). A test-wise student can pick the answer by tone without reading the letter. EVIDENCE: d91d6fb4 options 0–2 are padded throwaways; option 3 ("dreams come true through effort and sacrifice, not wishing") is the plain restatement of the page's thesis. — FIX: tighten distractor wording so length/register no longer signals the key; make at least one distractor a genuine misreading rather than a silly one.

[MINOR] unit-8-poem-believe-in-yourself › block#5/inline_quiz (2ca1065d rhyme item) — The rhyme-scheme answer "ABCB" is defensible but the question is presented as having one clean answer when the second line of each stanza is what rhymes; a thoughtful student counting the unrhymed first/third lines as "A/C" must infer the labelling convention. EVIDENCE: explanation: "ABCB — the second and fourth lines rhyme (see/be, fear/near, quo/grow, back/track)". This is correct, but "AABB" and "no rhyme" distractors are easy; the real confusion (ABAB vs ABCB) is the only live distractor, so the item is easier than its difficulty_level implies. — FIX: acceptable as-is; optionally note that only lines 2 and 4 rhyme so ABCB is the precise label.

The 26-item adaptive set and 18 apply-express items are otherwise clean: answer keys are correct, explanations cite the text, and the interpretation/inference items (ch8-pr-19 to ch8-pr-26) are genuinely open-reasoning rather than recall.
