# Chapter 9 review — Reading Skills: Unseen Passages

SUMMARY: This is a 2-page skills chapter (not a literary text), so source fidelity collapses to internal consistency — and on that count the two passages are arithmetically and factually self-consistent (the table sums to 200, the "one in seven" and "nearly doubled" claims check out, the word-count maths is right). The instructional pages are clear and the four-step method is sound. The real weakness sits in dimension C (assessment integrity): several MCQ distractors are throwaway nonsense rather than real misconceptions, the two assertion–reason items mislabel their own answer logic in ways that will actively confuse a careful student, and one A-R item omits the standard option set entirely. Dimension D shows the chapter leaning on the exact "happy secret / quiet power / the door" reveal-framing the banned-tell list warns against, repeated across both pages so the chapter reads machine-stamped. The single biggest systemic problem: the assertion–reason questions are pedagogically malformed — q4 on page 1 is logically backwards, and q4 on page 2 silently changes the answer-option scheme — so a student who actually understands A-R format gets misled.

## C. Assessment integrity (the focus dimension)
- [CRITICAL] reading-skills-intro › block#8 rs-d-q4 (assertion_reason) — the Reason does NOT explain the Assertion; the logic is backwards, yet it is marked `correct_index: 0` with the explanation claiming "R correctly explains A." Assertion: "A small habit is easier to begin than a big task." Reason: "The hardest part of any task is beginning it." If beginning is the hardest part of ANY task, that fact applies equally to small and big tasks — it does not, on its own, explain why a *small* habit is *easier* to begin. The passage's actual logic is that a small promise *shrinks the size of the beginning* ("if you promise just ten minutes, you sit down"). The stated Reason is a generic truth, not the cause of the Assertion. EVIDENCE: explanation: "because beginning is the hardest part (R), a tiny ten-minute promise removes the fear and gets you to sit down (A) — so R correctly explains A." The explanation smuggles in a third idea ("ten-minute promise removes the fear") that is in neither A nor R. FIX: rewrite R to "A small habit asks for only a tiny beginning, so the hardest part barely exists," or change the marked answer to "A true, R true, but R is not the correct explanation of A."
- [MAJOR] Both A-R items (rs-d-q4, rs-c-q4) expose no `options` array — only `correct_index`. EVIDENCE: rs-d-q4 has `"correct_index": 0` and no options field; rs-c-q4 has `"correct_index": 2` and no options field. The page-2 explanation refers to "the right choice is 'A is true, but R is false'" — an option string that appears nowhere in the block. A student is asked to pick index 2 of an option list they cannot see. This is a broken item as rendered unless the renderer injects a standard A-R option set; if it does, that standard set must be confirmed to match the index mapping (0,1,2,3) the explanations assume. FIX: emit the explicit four canonical A-R options on each item so index→meaning is unambiguous.
- [MAJOR] reading-skills-intro › block#8 rs-d-q3 — throwaway distractors that are not misconceptions: "saving money is more useful than reading", "habits cost a lot of money", "banks are safer than good habits". None is a plausible reading of "interest in a bank"; the correct option is findable by eliminating obvious nonsense without understanding the analogy. FIX: replace with near-miss distractors, e.g. "habits give instant results like a bank balance" (tests the *delay* the analogy hinges on).
- [MAJOR] reading-skills-case-based › block#2 rs-c-q3 — distractors "fell down to zero" and "became the highest activity of all" are silly; only "stayed exactly the same" vs "nearly doubled" is a real choice, and the passage states the answer verbatim. EVIDENCE: explanation: "it rose from 28 to 51 — nearly double." Answer is a direct restatement; no comprehension tested beyond locating a number.
- [MINOR] reading-skills-case-based › block#2 rs-c-q2 — option "None of them at all" is a throwaway given the passage explicitly says 28 read; "Exactly half" and "about one in three" are at least numerically themed, so this item is the strongest of the set but still leans on a giveaway ("the passage says this directly", per its own explanation).
- [MINOR] Explanations across both pages restate the option rather than teach (e.g. rs-c-q1 explanation: "the table shows 96 students chose watching videos / phone — the highest of the four"). Acceptable for a skills chapter but adds no interpretive value.
