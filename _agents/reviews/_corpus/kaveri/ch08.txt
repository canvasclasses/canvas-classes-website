{
 "reasoning_type": "logical",
 "difficulty_level": 3,
 "prompt": "The mother tells Ming to 'count the cost' — to honestly add up the years of effort, money, and sacrifice — *before* plunging into a dream. Isn't that the opposite of what we usually tell dreamers ('just go for it, don't think about the obstacles')? Why might 'count the cost first' be the *wiser* advice?",
 "reveal": "'Just go for it' sounds inspiring but can be cruel — it sends people leaping blindly, only to crash when the real costs appear, leaving them disillusioned and hurt. 'Count the cost first' is the advice of someone who *loves* the dreamer enough to be honest. It doesn't say *don't* dream; it says *dream with your eyes open.* By honestly facing the years and sacrifices in advance, you do two things: you weed out the dreams that are really just passing wishes (which is kind — better to know now), and you *strengthen* the dreams that survive the test, because you commit to them knowing exactly what they'll cost. A dream you've counted the cost of is far more durable than one built on excitement alone. That's why, right after 'count the cost', she adds the beautiful condition: '*if it is still burning in your blood*' — the costs are not meant to put out the fire, but to prove the fire is real. Honesty is not the enemy of dreams; it is their foundation."
}

 "reveal": "Antithesis works because the human mind understands things best by *contrast*. We grasp 'big' most sharply when it's next to 'small'; 'light' means most beside 'dark'.
