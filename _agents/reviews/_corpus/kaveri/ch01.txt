 "category": "tradition",
 "short_desc": "An Indian tradition where a younger person bows and touches the feet of an elder or teacher as a mark of respect.",
 "detail": "In Indian tradition, **touching someone's feet** (called *charan sparsh* or *namaskara*) is the highest expression of respect. It is normally done by the **young to the old**, by students to teachers, by the family to gods and elders. It is almost never done the other way. It is the grandmother saying: *here, in this moment, you are not my grandchild — you are my teacher.*",

 "variant": "ncert_intext",
 "reveal_mode": "tap_to_reveal",
 "problem": "Why do you think the grandmother felt embarrassed to ask someone else to read to her while the narrator was away?",
 "solution": "**A model answer:** The grandmother had been a respected elder all her life — her family was well-off, she had grandchildren who could read, and she had quietly carried the lack of schooling for sixty-two years. Embarrassment, in her case, was not vanity; it was the cost of pride that asked nothing of the world.\n\nThe text shows this directly in her own words: *'I could have asked somebody in this village but I was too embarrassed to do so.  "solution": "**A model answer:** The narrator is twelve.  "solution": "**A model answer:** *Kashi Yatre* plays **three roles** in this story:\n\n1. **The trigger.** The grandmother's emotional connection to Triveni's serial is what makes the week-long absence of a reader feel intolerable. Without *Kashi Yatre*, there is no reason for her to want to read.\n\n2. **A mirror.** The old lady in Triveni's novel also longs to go to Kashi and never gets there — yet she chooses something better than her own pilgrimage (an orphan girl's wedding).  "problem": "How effectively does the story highlight the value of education in supporting personal independence?",
 "solution": "**A model answer:** Very effectively — and the reason it works is that the story **never uses the word *education* at all.**\n\nInstead, it shows what the absence of literacy *feels* like: a 62-year-old woman in tears, rubbing her hands over a magazine she cannot decode, refusing to ask a neighbour because the asking would itself be a confession. {
 "markdown": "**Two patterns** in this lesson — both drawn from the story you just read.\n\nFirst, some English expressions always travel in pairs — *sink or swim, mix and match, hide and seek*. These are called **binomials**. The order is fixed. You can never say *seek and hide*.\n\nSecond, English flips the meaning of a word by attaching a small piece to the front — *happy → unhappy, fortune → misfortune*. That small piece is called a **prefix**. Both patterns reward you for noticing once and then reusing forever."
}
